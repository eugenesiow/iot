Linked Data Analytics on Lightweight Computers for the Internet of Things

About

The Internet of Things is currently beset by product silos. To unlock the commercial potential there is a need for open ecosystems based upon open standards. This includes standards for identification, discovery and interoperation of services across platforms from different vendors, and will involve the need for rich descriptions and shared data models, as well as close attention to security, privacy, scalability and accessibility.
WEB OF THINGS AT W3C

Applications and their utility to end users have been known to drive the adoption of technology, as West and Mace (2010) show with the iPhone and data services. For applications in the IoT to demonstrate the benefits of interconnecting entities and their data, there exist the challenges of

  1. aggregating & integrating multiple sources of data to derive insight and foresight from the data and
  2. creating an environment where entities can inter-operate and share data.

Hence the need to perform analytics on IoT data that uses open standards to model and describe the data for integration. Furthermore, this has to be performed on a platform suitable for the distributed, heterogenous nature of IoT deployments.

Lightweight computers, like the Raspberry Pi, are cost efficient and compact enough for broad or mobile deployments but possess significant storage and compute capabilities unlike sensors.

In IoT scenarios like Smart Homes, Smart Officies and Smart Cities, Lightweight Computers can serve as local platforms to perform integration and aggregation of data for analytics and applications. An additional benefit is fine-grained control over data privacy, protection and physical access by device owners.

Linked Data, which semantically interconnects structured data, has demonstrated its feasibility as a means of connecting and integrating web data using current infrastructure. 'Things' in the IoT, however, produce data that is markedly different and unique from Web data. This has to be handled differently to achieve good performance for analytics on the limited resources of Lightweight Computers.

The following projects, publications and research go towards this effort to provide powerful Linked Data solutions on distributed Lightweight Computers that allow data analytics for applications on the Internet of Things.

sparql2sql

The Internet of Things is a large and constantly growing source of time-series sensor data. Storing Internet of Things data as Linked Data in triple stores and using SPARQL to query them increases interoperability through the use and referencing of common identifiers and ontologies. Furthermore, the self-describing aspect of Linked Data is a boon for integration. However, storing sensor data in triple stores is often inefficient in terms of storage and query performance which is further exacerbated by the resource constraints of Lightweight Computers - compact & mobile machines that can be deployed in close proximity to sensors.

We propose an approach of storing time-series data, produced by sensors from the Internet of Things, more succinctly in relational tables that use a simple, bottom-up mapping approach to integrate and expose the data for SPARQL querying. We show that in comparison to storing the data in triple stores, our approach provides gains in both storage efficiency and query processing performance from over 3 times to over three orders of magnitude on Lightweight Computers. A range of analytical queries from a Linked Sensor Data benchmark, SRBench, on meteorological sensor data and analytical queries focusing on space-time-aggregations on Smart Home sensor data are tested.

The contribution of this research is to enable applications that utilise analytical queries to be built upon a performant framework that does not sacrifice interoperability.

Siow, E., Tiropanis, T., Hall, W.: Efficient SPARQL Queries for Analytics on Lightweight Computers for the Internet of Things (2015)

sparql2stream

The distributed and heterogeneous nature of the Internet of Things (IoT) drives the need for technologies that aggregate & integrate the data produced by physical entities like sensors. Linked Data, which semantically interconnects structured data, can be used on lightweight and mobile computers, deployed close to the source, to integrate data from sensors and devices with the added benefit of fine-grained control over data privacy. However, we show the performance limitations of a variety of systems on Lightweight Computers in inserting and querying Linked Data. We then design and demonstrate a solution that utilises stream processing, with an efficient row-based representation of time-series IoT data, and a translation engine to allow querying as SPARQL. We show this improves insertion and query performance by more than an order of magnitude, providing a case for Lightweight Computers with Linked Data for IoT data integration.

Siow, E., Tiropanis, T., Hall, W.: Linked Data on Lightweight Computers for the Internet of Things (2015)

A Survey on IoT Analytics

The Internet of Things (IoT) envisions a world-wide, interconnected network of smart physical entities. These physical entities generate a large amount of data in operation and as the IoT gains momentum in terms of deployment, the combined scale of those data seems destined to continue to grow. Increasingly, applications on the IoT involve analytics. Data analytics is the process of deriving knowledge from data, generating value like actionable insights from them. This article reviews work in the IoT and analytics from the perspective of their utility in creating efficient, effective and innovative applications and services for a wide spectrum of domains. We review the broad vision for the IoT as it is shaped in various communities, examine the application of data analytics by communities and provide a categorisation of analytic approaches in different types of settings in the IoT domain. This categorisation provides us with insights on the appropriateness of enabling infrastructures for IoT analytics. Finally, we examine the research challenges and opportunities going forward from the perspective of different stakeholders in the IoT.

Siow, E., Tiropanis, T., Hall, W.: Analytics for the Internet of Things: A Survey (2015)

IoT Observatories

An IoT Observatory System consists of an IoT Observatory that can be on a cluster or a computer/server, Observatory Nodes that can be hosted on computers or lightweight computers and sensors & devices that collect and harvest data from the environment.

The Web Observatory Project is developing a global, distributed infrastructure that allows the use and exchange of datasets, analytical apps and visualisations. The communities that it is fostering, through the network of Web Observatories that has emerged, are able to observe the web and explore its processes while developing an understanding of its impact on varying sectors of human activity. The foundation of the web of observatories that form the Web Observatory Project, are web observatory instances that exist as collections of datasets and analytical tools protected by access controls. The principles espoused by the Web Observatory as a distributed infrastructure are a possible solution for managing datasets and apps in the IoT, however, differences in IoT data and applications necessitate we differentiate IoT Observatories from Web Observatories.

'Things' in the IoT, produce data that is markedly different and unique from Web data. Whether you consider the perspectives of machine-to-machine (M2M) systems or smart objects that interact with humans and other objects, time-series sensor data are a common output from such devices. Unlike web data which ranges from documents and metadata to semantic and social media data, it can be argued that IoT data is characterised more by its volume and velocity than its variety (it is usually well-structured).

From various surveys that show the potential range of unique application domains that IoT applications can contribute to, there is a fundamental shift from human-human communication through the web to multi-partite communications that include machines in the picture, differentiating it from the Web when it comes to building applications.

The unique data, applications and challenges of the IoT discussed above provide motivation for building personal IoT datastores that are interoperable but also efficient at storing time-series sensor data and processing analytical queries. These datastores can then become nodes within distributed infrastructure called IoT Observatories that draw inspiration from Web Observatories and seek to be a means of engaging IoT communities to co-create, manage and share IoT resources (datasets, apps and infrastructure) while respecting privacy and access control.
